# Sources

The frozen bibliography. Each entry as it appears in the paper's `## References`, with a one-line provenance note. Every in-text `et al.` maps to a co-authored entry here (Tomasello et al. 2005 → full author list below).

## Bibliography

Adams, F., and Aizawa, K. (2008). *The Bounds of Cognition*. Blackwell. — the constitution-skeptic case; lets the thesis rest on explanatory unit, not metaphysics.

Boyd, R., and Richerson, P. J. (1985). *Culture and the Evolutionary Process*. University of Chicago Press. — cumulative culture as a ratchet no individual turns alone.

Carson, J. (2007). *The Measure of Merit: Talents, Intelligence, and Inequality in the French and American Republics, 1750–1940*. Princeton University Press. — intelligence as merit-machinery; the settlement edge.

Clark, A. (2008). *Supersizing the Mind: Embodiment, Action, and Cognitive Extension*. Oxford University Press. — the developed extended-mind case.

Clark, A., and Chalmers, D. (1998). The extended mind. *Analysis*, 58(1), 7–19. — Otto's notebook; the hybrid at its smallest.

Collins, H. (2010). *Tacit and Explicit Knowledge*. University of Chicago Press. — tacit knowledge as the skill of coupling.

Danziger, K. (1990). *Constructing the Subject: Historical Origins of Psychological Research*. Cambridge University Press. — how psychology built its individual unit.

Daston, L., and Galison, P. (2007). *Objectivity*. Zone Books. — trained scientific perception as institutional product.

Donald, M. (1991). *Origins of the Modern Mind: Three Stages in the Evolution of Culture and Cognition*. Harvard University Press. — external memory and the cultural staging of cognition.

Dreyfus, H. L. (1992). *What Computers Still Can't Do: A Critique of Artificial Reason*. MIT Press. — embodied expertise against rule-based AI.

Gallagher, S. (2005). *How the Body Shapes the Mind*. Oxford University Press. — embodied cognition.

Galison, P. (1997). *Image and Logic: A Material Culture of Microphysics*. University of Chicago Press. — knowledge as material-culture output of instruments.

Gould, S. J. (1981). *The Mismeasure of Man*. W. W. Norton. — reification of intelligence; the purification critique in miniature.

Hayles, N. K. (2017). *Unthought: The Power of the Cognitive Nonconscious*. University of Chicago Press. — prior usage of "cognitive assemblage," acknowledged.

Henrich, J. (2016). *The Secret of Our Success*. Princeton University Press. — collective brain; humans smart because cultural.

Heyes, C. (2018). *Cognitive Gadgets: The Cultural Evolution of Thinking*. Harvard University Press. — thinking skills as culturally evolved, not innate.

Hollan, J., Hutchins, E., and Kirsh, D. (2000). Distributed cognition: toward a new foundation for human-computer interaction research. *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 7(2), 174–196. — programmatic statement of distributed cognition.

Hutchins, E. (1995). *Cognition in the Wild*. MIT Press. — the navigation case; cognition as a property of the bridge.

Kirsh, D., and Maglio, P. (1994). On distinguishing epistemic from pragmatic action. *Cognitive Science*, 18(4), 513–549. — action that restructures a problem rather than advancing it; scaffolding as computation.

Knorr Cetina, K. (1999). *Epistemic Cultures: How the Sciences Make Knowledge*. Harvard University Press. — the machinery of fact-making and its disappearance from the fact.

Latour, B. (1987). *Science in Action: How to Follow Scientists and Engineers Through Society*. Harvard University Press. — inscriptions and networks.

Latour, B. (1993). *We Have Never Been Modern* (C. Porter, Trans.). Harvard University Press. — the purification/hybrid template the paper transposes.

Latour, B., and Woolgar, S. (1986). *Laboratory Life: The Construction of Scientific Facts*. Princeton University Press. — the laboratory as hybrid-maker and purifier.

Lave, J., and Wenger, E. (1991). *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation*. Cambridge University Press. — expertise installed through participation.

Malafouris, L. (2013). *How Things Shape the Mind: A Theory of Material Engagement*. MIT Press. — material engagement; thought happening in clay and wheel.

Netz, R. (1999). *The Shaping of Deduction in Greek Mathematics: A Study in Cognitive History*. Cambridge University Press. — the diagram as a device that makes deduction possible.

Noë, A. (2004). *Action in Perception*. MIT Press. — enactive perception.

Polanyi, M. (1966). *The Tacit Dimension*. University of Chicago Press. — we know more than we can tell.

Rotman, B. (2000). *Mathematics as Sign: Writing, Imagining, Counting*. Stanford University Press. — symbolic writing as cognitive technology; the mathematician as what the symbols do.

Rupert, R. D. (2004). Challenges to the hypothesis of extended cognition. *Journal of Philosophy*, 101(8), 389–428. — the systematicity case against literal extension.

Shapin, S. (1994). *A Social History of Truth: Civility and Science in Seventeenth-Century England*. University of Chicago Press. — the credit economy that resolves collective work into named authorship.

Sterelny, K. (2010). Minds: extended or scaffolded? *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences*, 9(4), 465–481. — scaffolding as the useful middle position.

Sterelny, K. (2012). *The Evolved Apprentice: How Evolution Made Humans Unique*. MIT Press. — apprentice learning and the built developmental environment.

Tomasello, M. (1999). *The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition*. Harvard University Press. — the human specialism is cultural learning.

Tomasello, M., Carpenter, M., Call, J., Behne, T., and Moll, H. (2005). Understanding and sharing intentions: the origins of cultural cognition. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 28(5), 675–691. — shared intentionality as the enrollment mechanism.

Varela, F. J., Thompson, E., and Rosch, E. (1991). *The Embodied Mind: Cognitive Science and Human Experience*. MIT Press. — enactive cognition; mind as embodied action.
